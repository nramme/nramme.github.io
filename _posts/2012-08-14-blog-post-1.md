“A species concept refers to the meaning of species in nature and to their role in the household of nature.” Ernst Mayr
--------

Speciation is a complex and continuous process, and it is not always straightforward to determine where to place discrete delimitations when the species in question are not evolutionarily distant enough for their differences to be evident. These differences may be morphological, genetic and/or ecological, and there are many different species concepts that diverge in criteria to delimit species, thus impacting the number of recognized species of a given group. For example, the Biological Species Concept (BSC) is based on reproductive isolation, whereas the Phylogenetic Species Concept is based on evidence of monophyly, and the Genetic Species Concept relies on sequence divergence (De Queiroz, 2007). The most known species concept is the BSC, but it does not apply to asexual organisms (Mayr, 1942, 2008; Dobzhansky, 1950). To circumvent the problem of several distinct species concepts, De Queiroz proposed the unified species concept (De Queiroz, 1998). Species might be defined as separately evolving metapopulation lineages, and reproductive isolation is just one of the operational criteria for species delimitation, along with other characteristics such as different occupation of niches, monophyly and morphological distinguishability (De Queiroz, 1998, 2007). These are all properties that lineages may acquire during their evolution and might be considered lines of evidence for species delimitation (De Queiroz, 1998, 2007).

The delimitation of a species cannot be limited to a single metric
---------

Why is genetic difference alone not sufficient for species distinction? For instance, some species may contain much more genetic diversity than others. Organisms may also show great overall genomic similarity, but have a set of genes providing them the ability to conquer other niches, increase the range of hosts in the case of parasitic organisms, and disperse through other environments. For this reason, contemporary taxonomists often use an integrative approach that does not rely on a single metric and gathers as much evidence as possible for species delimitation, taking into account genetic, ecological, phylogenetic and morphological data (Hawksworth and Lücking, 2017; Zhao et al., 2018; Košuthová et al., 2020).

A correct species identification and classification is the first step for studying biodiversity, including how many species exist, where they are distributed, and how they evolve, specialize, change hosts, and occupy new niches. Understanding where one species ends and another begins is therefore not merely a taxonomic exercise. Species boundaries influence how we study biodiversity, reconstruct evolutionary history, and understand the emergence of infectious diseases. In parasites, changes in host range can have important consequences for disease transmission and public health, making species delimitation a central question in parasitology. Population genetics and comparative genomics provide powerful tools to investigate these processes, helping identify the genetic basis of adaptation to new hosts and environments.
